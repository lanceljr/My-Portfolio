Why this mattered

Some business figures have been omitted or generalised per confidentiality obligations.

Latin America is one of the world's fastest-growing regions for crypto adoption. According to Chainalysis, the region recorded over $562 billion in on-chain transaction volume in 2023 ‚Äî driven by high stablecoin usage in high-inflation economies and growing retail participation across Mexico, Colombia, and Brazil. Argentina, Venezuela, and Brazil consistently rank in the global top 15 for crypto adoption per capita.

The macro case was strong: LatAm has a large underbanked population, over $150 billion in annual remittances (where crypto offers a cheaper and faster alternative), and high smartphone penetration ‚Äî all driving demand for accessible digital financial tools.

80% of our LatAm revenue came from just three countries: Mexico, Argentina, and Colombia. Argentina was the standout ‚Äî local currency instability drives consistent demand for dollar-denominated digital assets. Colombia was the fastest growing.

But we were losing market share to competitors who had already solved what we hadn't. The market leader had 6√ó our trading volume in Mexico and Colombia. In Argentina the gap was smaller, but a single competitor's Argentina revenue was still nearly 3√ó ours. Every major player offered instant bank transfers. We didn't. That one gap was compounding.

ii.

Problem

To start investing on our platform, new users had to first fund their account. In LatAm, the only option was P2P trading ‚Äî a five-step manual process requiring interaction with a stranger, payment proof uploads, and a manual fund release. For most first-time users, this felt like too much to get through before ever placing a trade.

Both steps were the problem.

Research & decisions

I needed to understand why P2P wasn't working for new users.

üìä
What the data showed

First-deposit completion was below market because P2P was the only way to fund an account in LatAm ‚Äî there was no alternative path for new users. And it wasn't just a deposit problem: less than 20% of users who completed their first deposit made a repeated trade within a month. We were losing users both at the deposit step and right after it.

üó£
50+ user interviews ‚Äî MX ¬∑ CO ¬∑ AR

A clear pattern emerged: new users found P2P difficult to navigate and intimidating. Many had switched to using Bitso or other local exchanges just to onramp, then transferred funds back to us. We weren't the front door anymore ‚Äî users were routing around us to get in.

üèÜ
Competitive landscape

Every major regional player had already solved this. Bitso ran on SPEI. Binance used local rails. Lemon and Ripio offered direct bank deposits. While they gave new users a frictionless path from local currency to crypto, we were asking ours to navigate a peer-to-peer marketplace first.

The direction was clear: we needed a native bank transfer flow. The real question was how ‚Äî without rebuilding the rails ourselves. Three decisions defined the approach.

Decision 1 ‚Äî Build vs. Partner
Building our own fiat rails would mean banking licenses and regulatory approvals in each market ‚Äî easily 12‚Äì18 months per country. We chose to partner with an existing payments aggregator and reinvested that time into the user experience instead.
Decision 2 ‚Äî One partner, five markets
Rather than integrating a separate provider per country, we found a single partner with pre-existing coverage across Argentina, Mexico, Peru, Chile, and Ecuador. One integration unlocked five markets ‚Äî and avoided rebuilding compliance, settlement, and FX conversion from scratch in each.
Decision 3 ‚Äî Run compliance and engineering in parallel
Country licensing and payment processor contracts were the longest-lead dependencies on the project. Rather than treating them as a gate, I pulled compliance into the discovery phase so legal and engineering tracks ran concurrently. Mexico and Colombia launched together; Argentina followed 8 weeks later.
iv.

What we built

A country-aware deposit flow powered by a global payments partner as the aggregator. The mechanic: the partner accepts a local bank transfer, converts it into a digital dollar, and credits the user's account ‚Äî automatically, in one flow.

  • Mexico: SPEI (Mexico's real-time bank transfer system ‚Äî money arrives in under 30 seconds) as the default. OXXO cash deposit available for users without a bank account.
  • Colombia: PSE as the default ‚Äî the same bank network already used by Bancolombia, Nequi, and Daviplata customers daily.
  • Argentina: Standard bank transfer and Mercado Pago wallet (Argentina's most widely-used digital payment app).

We also introduced a lightweight identity verification tier for first deposits under $500. This cut initial setup from ~7 minutes to ~90 seconds for most new users, removing the last major friction point before a first deposit.

Fees were surfaced upfront in the confirmation screen ‚Äî no surprises at the end. On every primary payment method, our fee matched or beat the leading local competitors.

Reflection

Two things I'd carry into the next one.

üó∫
Map the plumbing before you pick a partner

QUICK CONTEXT ¬∑ WHAT IS A P2P MARKETPLACE?

A P2P (peer-to-peer) marketplace lets users buy and sell crypto directly with each other using their local currency and bank rails. The exchange acts as escrow, not the counterparty ‚Äî one user posts an offer, another fills it, and the platform releases the crypto once payment is confirmed. In markets where card on-ramps are expensive or fiat rails are restricted, P2P is often the primary way users get into crypto.

Vietnam was already our second-largest market globally ‚Äî yet on trading volume, we were still trailing the regional category leaders. That gap wasn't a sign of weak demand; it was a sign of how much headroom was still on the table. If we could close even part of it, the payoff at this scale was substantial.

Indonesia and the Philippines had been left behind for a while ‚Äî we hadn't meaningfully invested in either market. But the market research was hard to argue with: large young populations, mobile-first behaviour, high crypto interest, and competitors already pushing in. The growth potential in both was huge. Together with Vietnam, these were the three SEA markets I focused on first ‚Äî standing still wasn't an option.

ii.

Problem

The headline number was simple: first deposits via P2P were weak. But the actual problem only made sense once we drilled into why ‚Äî and what we found wasn't a funnel leak, it was a marketplace dynamic.

üìâ 01 ¬∑ OBSERVED First-deposit conversion via P2P sat well below benchmark.

Users were signing up and completing KYC ‚Äî then not funding their account through us. The drop wasn't a UX problem; it was happening after they'd already chosen to be customers.

üîç 02 ¬∑ BEHAVIOUR They were buying elsewhere and transferring crypto in to sell.

A consistent pattern surfaced: users bought crypto on competitor exchanges, then moved the asset over to us to sell. We were getting volume ‚Äî but mostly as a sell-side venue, losing the buy-side flow that should have been ours.

üí± 03 ¬∑ WHY Why? Our P2P rates weren't competitive.

Buyers got a worse price on our marketplace than they did on regional category leaders. In a fungible product like crypto, even a small spread is enough to send users elsewhere.

‚öôÔ∏è 04 ¬∑ ROOT CAUSE And rates weren't competitive because liquidity was too thin.

Without enough takers to make ads worth posting, merchants couldn't quote aggressive prices. Thin liquidity meant wider spreads, which pushed buyers out, which left us with even thinner liquidity. A classic cold-start marketplace stuck in reverse.

Drawn out, the marketplace was stuck in the wrong half of its own flywheel ‚Äî every turn made the next turn worse:

üíß Low liquidity ‚Üí üìà Wider spreads ‚Üí üí∏ Worse rates ‚Üë ‚Üì üìã Merchants stop posting ‚Üê üë§ Fewer takers ‚Üê üö™ Users buy elsewhere
‚Üª Marketplace flywheel ‚Äî running in reverse

The problem wasn't activation as a number ‚Äî it was a cold-start marketplace. So the brief I set was specific: be aggressive on incentives to subsidise the rate gap, get takers in volume, and keep them coming back ‚Äî not through one-off promos, but through a repeated, programmatic mechanic. More takers would attract more merchants to post ads; more merchants would tighten spreads; tighter spreads would pull more organic demand. The flywheel just needed something to spin it.

iii.

Developing the idea

The flywheel diagnosis defined the brief: a mechanic that could subsidise the rate gap, reward the behaviours that fed liquidity, and pull users back often enough for the loop to compound. The four chapters below walk through how I moved from that brief to a concrete product spec ‚Äî define the mechanic, validate it, segment the user base, set the build principles.

A

Defining the mechanic

What kind of incentive layer would actually move the flywheel.

Context

Vietnam ranked top-5 globally for crypto adoption; Indonesia and the Philippines were top-10 (Chainalysis 2023). We held ~20% market share across these markets ‚Äî meaningful, but behind regional leaders with multi-year head starts on brand and liquidity.

The product wasn't the gap. Across several rounds of merchant interviews and surveys, the feedback came back consistent: the app was solid, but takers wouldn't trade with us because our prices weren't the most competitive and other exchanges always had more campaigns running.

"Your app is fine. But buyers compare prices first ‚Äî if I quote at my floor, I still lose to other exchanges most of the time."

‚Äî P2P merchant, Vietnam

‚Äî P2P merchant, Indonesia

‚Äî Taker survey respondent, Philippines

Approach

We weren't lacking in product features ‚Äî we were lacking in reasons to come back and reasons to stay cheaper. So instead of benchmarking crypto peers, I studied the consumer-tech apps SEA users were already fluent in ‚Äî Shopee, Grab, Tokopedia. Two mechanics surfaced as the load-bearing retention pattern across all three:

üõí
Shopee ‚Äî vouchers

Stackable, time-bound vouchers issued for daily login, first purchase, and category-specific actions. The wallet is a daily destination; expiry creates the return loop.

üõµ
Grab ‚Äî points + rewards

GrabRewards points earned on every transaction, redeemable for discount vouchers, partner deals, and tier perks. Behaviour in, reward out ‚Äî every ride builds toward the next claim.

Finding

The mechanics differed (vouchers vs. points, expiry vs. tier progression) but the underlying pattern was identical: an incentive layer wrapped around the core transaction, turning each interaction into a small commitment loop ‚Äî do this, get this, come back to claim more. Different product, same growth psychology.

B

Validating the hypothesis

A platform-scale build needs validation before scoping. I pulled it from our own data and direct user interviews.

Approach

The mechanic looked right on paper. Before committing to a multi-market platform build, I wanted to validate it against our own user behaviour and direct interview feedback ‚Äî not just analogy to adjacent consumer-tech.

01 / DATA
Activation collapses after the first week

The majority of users who eventually traded did so within the first 7 days of signup. Beyond that window, users were 5√ó less likely to ever trade. Pre-launch activation in SEA averaged 19% across VN/ID/PH ‚Äî and email/push reactivation barely moved the number, because there was no real incentive for users to come back.

02 / INTERVIEWS
Users were already rate-shopping

8 in-depth interviews + 412 survey responses (VN/ID/PH). >80% held accounts on 2+ exchanges and rate-shopped per trade. 64% cited rates / fees as the top decision factor on a given day. 41% of new users said a promo or referral nudged their first deposit. The behaviour we needed already existed ‚Äî vouchers had to win those decision moments.

03 / CONTEXT
SEA users are already trained on this

SEA's largest consumer apps ‚Äî Shopee, Tokopedia, Grab ‚Äî all build retention around vouchers and points programs (Bain & Google e-Conomy SEA 2023). Users in our research expected, not just tolerated, the mechanic: time-limited claims, stackable rewards, visible expiry. The habit was already trained.

04 / SYNTHESIS
Vouchers fit ‚Äî as a loop, not a discount

The mechanic was a fit, with one caveat: it only compounds if wired as a behaviour-triggered loop. As a one-off promo it produces a spike and decays. As triggered, owned infrastructure it builds a habit.

Conclusion

Vouchers were the right mechanic. The question moved from whether to how ‚Äî specifically, how to deploy them when the same voucher means very different things to different users.

C

Segmenting users by lifecycle stage

A single voucher couldn't do the right job across three taker-side segments.

Initial design

First sketch was a one-off discount campaign ‚Äî a single market-wide voucher drop to subsidise the rate gap. It would ship, but I wasn't confident on ROI: a flat discount sent to everyone treats a new user and a lapsed trader the same, and the spend doesn't compound after the window closes.

Research method

I scoped the study to takers ‚Äî the side that drives volume growth and where subsidy economics actually work ‚Äî and ran cohort analysis on 12 months of internal data alongside stratified user interviews (n=24 across VN/ID/PH, sampled across new / lapsed / active sub-tier) to see whether takers behaved as one cohort or several.

Finding ¬∑ what each segment needed

Three taker-side segments fell out of the data, each with a distinct job-to-be-done:

üÜï New users

"I downloaded it, but I'm not sure what to buy first ‚Äî and the price seems higher than other apps."

Need: low-friction trigger to make a first deposit; subsidy must close the rate gap visibly.

üò¥ Lapsed

"I haven't opened it in a while. I forgot it was there until the notification came."

Need: re-entry trigger tied to dormancy threshold ‚Äî a discount alone doesn't recall the app.

‚ö° Active ¬∑ sub-tier

"I trade on OKX occasionally, because it doesnt have any fees. But sometimes I go somewhere else cause its cheaper also."

Need: volume-tier rebate that compounds with usage; price-sensitive but already engaged.

Why a rule engine ‚Äî not multiple campaigns

Each segment needed its own trigger, voucher type, and eligibility rule ‚Äî and we needed to ship across VN, ID, and PH. Running this as parallel campaigns meant a code change for every tweak: iteration speed wouldn't keep up with what we'd learn in-market.

A rule engine fixed that. Set the trigger, the voucher, and the budget in a config ‚Äî the engine handles the rest. Growth ships new rules without engineering, and the same setup works for any market.

Same engine. Different rules per segment, per market, per campaign. Adding a new rule is config, not code.

D

Setting product principles

Three product calls that shaped how the engine got built.

Each principle was a deliberate trade-off against a real alternative ‚Äî and each is the place a worse version of this project would have taken the easier path.

01
Build an engine, not a campaign

Marketing wanted a single seasonal promo for SEA. I pushed back and scoped a rules-based voucher engine instead ‚Äî supporting fee discounts, USDT bonuses, and cashback rebates as configurable voucher types. Same engineering effort, durable infrastructure.

02
Trigger on behaviour, not signup

Issuing vouchers at signup attracts deal-hunters with low intent. We tied issuance to specific behaviours ‚Äî first deposit, first trade, dormancy thresholds ‚Äî so each voucher both rewarded an action and nudged the next one. This also kept unit economics honest.

03
Region-agnostic from day one

Eligibility rules, voucher types, expiry windows, value caps, and issuance triggers were all configuration, not code. A regional growth lead could spin up a new campaign without filing an engineering ticket. This was the call that turned a SEA project into global infrastructure.

With those calls locked, the build scope was concrete: rules-based issuance, an in-app surface where the discount gets used, and region-level configuration owned by growth ‚Äî packaged as a platform capability, not a campaign.

iv.

What we built

A voucher engine designed as a platform capability ‚Äî three components, one shared infrastructure, owned by growth teams directly.

PART 01
Issuance service

Rules-based issuance triggered by user behaviour (first deposit, first trade, X days dormant), filtered by eligibility segments (region, KYC tier, cohort), capped by budget. All configurable.

PART 02
In-app voucher centre (within P2P)

A dedicated voucher centre inside the P2P marketplace, surfacing active vouchers, expiry countdowns, and progress toward claim conditions ‚Äî right next to the order screen where the discount actually gets used. Expiry push notifications close the loop.

PART 03
Region config layer

Eligibility rules, voucher types, expiry windows, value caps ‚Äî all configuration. A new market goes live by setting up rules and budget. No engineering ticket required.

The same engine handles trading-fee discounts, USDT/crypto bonuses, and cashback rebates ‚Äî growth teams can mix voucher types within a single campaign.

Merchants on our P2P marketplace were already fighting for the top of the list. We weren't charging for it. I identified the opportunity, designed the guardrails that made it fair, and shipped the first auction-based sponsored listing product in crypto P2P history.

i.

Background

Some business figures have been omitted or generalised per confidentiality obligations.

Paid placement is one of the oldest mechanics in digital commerce. We were sitting on it and not charging for it.

Google built a $200B+ business on a simple insight: the top of a search results page is the most valuable real estate on the internet. Businesses pay to appear there because that's where the clicks go. Amazon runs the same playbook in product discovery ‚Äî sponsored listings at the top, organic results below. The model is universal: in any ranked list, attention concentrates at the top, and concentrated attention can be monetised.

Our P2P marketplace works the same way. Takers open the app, see a list of merchants offering to buy or sell crypto, and pick from near the top. There's no algorithm surfacing a "best match" ‚Äî just a ranked list, and rank determines volume. A merchant at position 1 sees far more trades than one at position 5. That's the same structural dynamic as Google Search and Amazon ‚Äî just applied to a crypto exchange.

Our merchants already understood this intuitively. They were fighting for the top position constantly ‚Äî refreshing rates, monitoring their rank, pulling listings when pushed down. The demand for paid visibility was real and measurable. We just weren't charging for it.

iii.

Problem

"If we let merchants buy the top spot ‚Äî what stops them from exploiting the takers who click on them?"

This was the question that had to be answered before anything else. Paid placement in a two-sided marketplace has a well-known failure mode: the party that pays can capture visibility at the expense of the party that doesn't. In retail it's sponsored results that are worse products. In P2P, it would be sponsored merchants offering bad rates to takers who reasonably assumed the top result was the best deal.

Two failure modes shaped every design decision that followed:

Failure mode A
Pay-to-win

Rich merchants dominate the top regardless of rate quality. Takers lose. Merchant trust erodes. The marketplace degrades.

Failure mode B
Rate exploitation

Sponsored merchants post uncompetitive rates. Takers trust the top result and get a bad deal. NPS tanks. Regulatory risk rises.

The product could only exist if both failure modes were designed against structurally ‚Äî not as policy or moderation, but baked into the core mechanics.

Three guardrails. Each one addressed a specific way the system could break.

Merchants bid in USDT per 12-hour slot. The highest qualifying bids win. We also capped the number of slots any single merchant could hold per week ‚Äî so smaller merchants always have a chance to participate, and no one can lock out the field indefinitely.
